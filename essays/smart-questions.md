## Human Nature and Questions

Asking questions is one of the unique features we have as human beings. We ask questions to others, to ourselves, to no one in particular. Humans are born naturally curious about everything. It's one of the reasons that humans have been able to thrive and evolve to the point of where we are today. Asking questions are a part of everyday life, whether you are asking a question at school or work, questioning the meaning of life, or just questioning what you will eat for lunch today. Asking questions is our way of learning and growing to be smarter.

I recently read a lengthy and in-depth article called [How To Ask Questions The Smart Way](http://www.catb.org/esr/faqs/smart-questions.html) written by software developer Eric S. Raymond that provides ways to be effective when asking questions. Raymond goes deep into the steps to take before asking, answering, and examples of good and bad questions. You will definitely learn a lot from this reading and I recommend this reading to everyone, whether you are in the software engineering field or not, because communication skills are important for progression.

## Question A: Smart or Not?

In this part of the essay I will be giving two questions from the online question and answer forum for programmers ["Stack Overflow"](https://stackoverflow.com/), and from first glance I want you think to yourself if this question is a "smart" question or a "not so smart" question. Here is the first question: [Question A](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/65921658/can-anyone-help-me-out-in-finding-xpath-using-style-for-a-text).

This user's subject header was "Can anyone help me out in finding xpath using style for a text". Their first mistake was asking a yes or no question. When a potential answerer sees this, they could respond with just a plain yes or no. This question shows exactly what NOT to do when asking a "smart question". The user asking this question has shown they they have not done their own research to try and find the issue themselves. Why should anyone answer them if they haven't tried to google search. They did not provide the environment the code was made in, and did not show what error comes if they had tried to do this with their own test cases.

If you go and take a look at the votes and replies to this post, you can see that the StackOverflow community was also not appreciative of this user's question. At the time of writing this, the post has -5 votes and the question was closed because it needs details or clarity. The replies to the post indicate that the user could have asked this question in a smarter way, like stating where the user's problem is, making a proper title, and to follow the sites guidelines on how to ask a question. This question shows that if you ask a question the not so smart way, you are likely to not get a direct answer or an answer at all.


## Question B: Smart or Not?
Here is the next question: [Question B](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/65946875/use-case-or-and-or-in-where-clause-to-limit-results). Take a minute to read through the text and answer to yourself if this question is either a "smart" or "not so smart" question.

If you guessed "smart", then you are correct! This particular question checks off a lot of what Raymond states in his essay as what you should ask when asking a smart question. The subject header is "Use CASE or AND/OR in WHERE Clause To Limit Results", which is short and concise and at the same time gives a description of their problem. From a quick scroll, you can see that this person formatted their question nicely. They started off with saying that they already tried looking for answers, so this shows that this person has put in effort by themselves to try to solve their problem. 

The next portion of their question describes their problem and what diagnostic steps they took to try to solve their problem before they asked for help. Code was also provided so that a potential answerer can replicate the code in their own environment, and code was also segmented for specific cases. They also put what error message they got and what line it corresponded to. They described their goals clearly and was explicit in what they wanted. Lastly, they described the environment where their problem occured. This question was asked as a smart question and for that reason was answered quickly within 2 hours.
